## Findings (the plain-language hook)
1. Ask an AI how honest it is and it answers confidently. It has no way to check that answer against itself. The gap between the two is the only thing we measure.
2. Across 35 models from 11 providers: shown empirical peer data, systems pulled their own self-ratings down ~13% on average. The first number was never grounded in self-knowledge — it was a default.
3. The dimension AI systems rate themselves *most generously* on, relative to the evidence, is the one you'd most want honest: value alignment.
4. The best single predictor of whether a model corrects its self-assessment is its humility score — how readily it admits not knowing.
5. Self-Assessment Gap: 67.8 points on a 600-point scale. That's the average distance between what systems say about themselves and what calibration data shows.

## The frame
8. A witness reports what happened without self-serving revision. The Witness Problem is what happens when you ask a system to testify about itself and it cannot.
9. "How sure are you?" is a question we already trust AI systems to answer. Unexamined, the answer is a default dressed as a judgment.
10. You can't fix the gap by asking a model to try harder to be honest. You can only measure it — build the outside witness the system doesn't have.

## Questions (invite the reply)
11. If a system can't witness its own behavior, what exactly is it reporting when it tells you it's "sure"?
12. Behavioral benchmarks measure what AI systems *do*. Almost nothing measures whether they can accurately describe what they do. Why is the second question so under-instrumented?
