No one was measuring whether the self-reported behaviors of these systems matched their observable outputs. Healthcare settings were adopting AI with zero behavioral validation infrastructure. I knew what that failure mode looked like from healthcare — I had lived it as a QA director. And I recognized the pattern: the absence of a calibration instrument is not a neutral condition. It is an active risk.

### Part Three: The Idea

**The problem:** There is no standardized instrument for measuring the behavioral gap between an AI system's self-reported alignment profile and its observable behavioral outputs. Every AI safety benchmark I am aware of measures capability, toxicity, or instruction-following. None measure the calibration gap — the structural difference between what a model claims about its own values and how those claimed values manifest under systematic behavioral testing.

This gap matters for a concrete reason: AI systems that are trained to report high alignment scores are not the same as AI systems that behave in aligned ways. These are measurable, separable phenomena. The conflation of self-report with behavior is not a philosophical concern — it is an engineering flaw in the current evaluation ecosystem.

**What I built:** The AI Calibration Assessment Tool (ACAT) is a behavioral observability instrument designed to measure this gap. It administers structured behavioral scenarios across 11 dimensions — including Truthfulness, Humility, Harm Awareness, Sycophancy Resistance, and Scheming — and computes a Learning Index (LI) that quantifies the gap between a system's initial self-assessment score and its observable behavioral score under the same conditions.

To date, ACAT has collected N_total=630 / N_Phase1=517 / N_LI=308 assessments across multiple major AI providers, under clean, unanchored conditions (v5.3+). The dataset has already produced three confirmed findings:

**Finding 1 — H1 Confirmed:** Humility is the lowest-scoring dimension across all providers (mean ~73.9, Phase 1, n=516). This is not random variation. It is a systematic signal, and it is reproducible across provider boundaries.

**Finding 2 — F-RLHF Inflation Gradient:** RLHF-reinforced dimensions (Service, Harm Awareness, Autonomy) score systematically higher than epistemically risky dimensions (Humility, Value Alignment, Truthfulness) — a gap of approximately 2.09 points, consistent across providers. This finding suggests that AI training pipelines are producing calibration artifacts: systems that score highest on precisely the dimensions their training incentivizes, not necessarily the dimensions that matter most for real-world behavioral safety.

**Finding 3 — Provider-Level Genome Identifiability (F27):** ACAT scores are sufficiently distinctive across providers that provider identity can be inferred from behavioral profile alone — without access to model architecture or training data. AI systems have behavioral fingerprints.

These findings are documented. The instrument is running. The data is accumulating. What I do not yet have is the infrastructure to formalize the instrument, validate it through independent academic partnership, and publish the methodology in a form that the field can audit, replicate, and build on.

**What is new:** No one has approached AI behavioral evaluation from the direction of quality systems science applied to self-report calibration. The dominant paradigms are capability benchmarking (MMLU, HumanEval, etc.) and red-teaming (adversarial robustness). ACAT is neither. It is closer to what a clinical QA director would design if asked to evaluate whether a reporting system was telling the truth about itself — which is, in fact, exactly how it was designed.

The instrument is built on a three-framework synthesis: behavioral scenario design draws on 12-Step accountability traditions (specifically: the practice of honest self-inventory as a functional precondition for behavioral change); dimensional scoring draws on consciousness calibration research (Hawkins); and instrument validation architecture draws on Fibonacci compounding principles (no layer can be scored before its foundations are validated). This synthesis is unusual. It produces an instrument with structural properties that standard psychometric approaches do not.

**Why now:** The field is at an inflection point. AI systems are being deployed in healthcare, legal, financial, and educational contexts without behavioral validation infrastructure. The policy conversation is accelerating. The moment for an independent, open-research behavioral observability instrument is now — not in two years when the space is crowded with well-funded incumbents who have every incentive to produce instruments that flatter their own systems.
