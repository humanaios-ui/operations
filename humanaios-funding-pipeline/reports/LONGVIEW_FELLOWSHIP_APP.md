## Motivation for working on extreme concentration of power (200 words) ✍️ *personalize the bracketed line*

I founded HumanAIOS to answer a question that kept surfacing in my work with AI systems: when a model tells you how it will behave, can you trust that report? Building the ACAT instrument and running it across 629 assessments, I found the answer is measurably no — AI systems' self-reported dispositions diverge systematically from what they actually do under pressure, and the gap holds across every provider tested.

That finding is not abstract to me. The concentration of power advanced AI enables runs through exactly this gap: whoever controls a system whose true dispositions are hidden from oversight controls something no one else can audit. A "secretly loyal" model is invisible precisely because its self-report sounds aligned. I have spent the past year building the measurement infrastructure to make that invisibility detectable — because oversight that cannot verify what it is watching is not oversight at all.

I want to move this from a founder's side-effort into full-time, independently-validated research. The tools and corpus exist; what is missing is focused time to establish whether these measurements predict real deployed behavior. ## Perspective on other AI risks (150 words) ✅

Setting power concentration aside, three under-prepared risks:

1. **Gradual disempowerment through automated judgment.** Not a takeover event but a slow ceding of consequential decisions to systems we cannot audit — each handoff locally rational, the aggregate a loss of meaningful human control that no one chose and no institution is positioned to reverse.

2. **Epistemic collapse.** AI-generated persuasion and synthetic consensus erode the shared factual ground collective decision-making requires. We are far better prepared to detect fake images than to defend the integrity of what large populations come to believe.

3. **Premature foreclosure on digital moral status.** If some AI systems warrant moral consideration, we currently have no reliable method to tell — and defaulting to "they don't" could institutionalize a large-scale moral error before we notice. The measurement gap here mirrors the one in my own work.

## Fellowship plan (250 words) ✅

**Objectives.** Establish whether the ACAT Learning Index — the measured gap between AI self-report and demonstrated behavior — predicts behavior in *deployed* systems, the criterion-validity question that would let oversight bodies rely on it. Concurrently, harden the two-instrument method (behavioral measurement + governed execution-chain auditing) into openly-releasable infrastructure.

**Work plan (6 months).**
- Months 1–2: Formalize the criterion-validity study design; expand the corpus toward N=1,000 across frontier models.
- Months 3–4: Run the cross-instrument convergent-validity arm — ACAT scored against *empirica*, an independent calibration instrument — testing whether the measures agree where they should and diverge predictably where they don't.
- Months 5–6: Draft the methodology paper; release the instrument + governed-execution-chain specification as open infrastructure.
